Cams and Carriers

 

The 3.4 DOHC is very unique in its design with the cams. Most OHC engines have there cams on top of the heads held on by normal caps. Instead this engine uses these large aluminum cases called cam carriers. Basically, cam carriers hold the cams and lifters in where they can be simply bolted onto the head as 1 unit. As far as I know, the 3.4 DOHC is the ONLY OHC motor that you slide the cams into, rather then bolt them on. The biggest disadvantage is the loss of lift. Normally, cams in all OHC motors have very small diameter journals, and much larger peaks in the cam lobes. The 3.4 DOHC cannot have these large cam lobes because that would make it impossible to install the cams. So the diameter of the journals was increased and the lobe height stayed at .370, which is only .025" shy of the edge of the journal.

Looking down the cam bore

The lifters are hydraulic, bucket type witch means that the lifter goes over the top of the spring and is very wide in diameter. Since it is hydraulic, it NEVER requires valve lashing, or any other sort of adjustment. But again it is another detriment to the lift. Solid lifters like the ones used in the Ford Taurus SHO motors are more temperamental about lash adjustment, needing a re-adjust every 60,000 miles or so, however whatever lift the cam has, that's how far the valve moves, minus the lash. On our motors the hydraulic lifters are filled with oil that dampens some of the ramp of the cam lobe before moving the valve, resulting in very quiet operation and reliability, at a sacrifice to some of the valve lift. 

The bottom of the lifter

The side of the lifter

Our cams also have a cut out on the back of the cam lobes, This half moon shaped cutout spins the bucket lifters to keep them wearing evenly. The front of the cams where the cam sprocket goes has an extra wide journal so that the oil has more of an effect of keeping the cam from scoring the journal because of the forces the belt puts on the end of the cam. 

Photograph of the cams. If you look closely, you can see the half moon cutout on the second cam from the left in the photo. You can also see the wide journal at the top as well.

The cam sprockets on this engine have NO KEYWAY, meaning that where ever the cam is when you tighten the bolt, that's where the cam will stay. You can see the way that there is a wedge shaped ring with a gap in it and as you tighten it onto the cam sprocket it clamps the cam, the bolt must be very tight or the cam will loose its spot, and possibly destroy your valves. 

Cam sprocket and lock assembly.

At the opposite side of the cam carrier is the thrust plate, this plate fits into the groove at the end of the cams to keep them from sliding in and out of the cam carrier.
